01
The problem

Employees navigated wikis, forms, and Slack to figure out what they were allowed to buy. Wrong answers aren't just frustrating — they have real compliance and financial consequences.

02
The critical decision

We rejected a pure chatbot. Procurement isn't a Q&A problem — it's a workflow problem. The assistant needed to know the rules, cite its sources, and hand people off to the right next step in the existing system.

03
The system

Intake Agent is the front door of a connected procurement loop. Compliance rules shape what the assistant says. Answers route users into catalog, forms, or approvals. Usage analytics flow back to help admins improve those rules. One thread — the whole procurement system behind it.

04
Shipped

Two major releases (2024–2026). Policy-grounded answers with sources, in-chat catalog and forms, admin routing table, test sandbox, and analytics.

01

The problem

What research made clear and where it pointed

Too many doors, not enough answers

People asked the same questions over and over. Answers were scattered across tools and Slack—slow to find and easy to get wrong.

Research & Insights

What research showed

  • 📊
    Internal support teams said half their week was repeat “how do I…?” questions.
  • 📚
    Written guides go out of date quickly; training everyone again doesn’t scale.
  • 🔒
    Wrong buying guidance is costly—people wanted proof (where an answer came from), not vibes.
02

Exploration

What we tried, what we ruled out, and what held up

Rejected direction

Form-first

Structured forms worked when users already knew what they needed. For everyone else, choosing a request type on step one was a dead end.

  • New hires didn't know which category to pick
  • No way to ask "is this allowed?" before committing to a path
  • Radio buttons assumed request types were fixed and knowable
  • Same rigid structure regardless of request complexity

Requisition is a governed workflow — not a chatbot brief. Chat-style surfaces couldn’t keep answers in policy context or hand off the next real step with confidence.

  • Policy, catalog, and approvals — not one-off Q&A
  • Weak ties to sources and deterministic routing
  • Jargon and tool knowledge before useful help
  • Little bridge into forms, catalog, or approvals

One entry point built for everyone. Admin-curated shortcuts surface repeat tasks. Role-tuned suggestions load on arrival.

The system

Admin side first — the rules that make the user experience possible

Admin experience

What admins configure

The assistant is only as good as its rules. Admins upload official docs (grounding answers in real policy), set routing per topic, define when the assistant must stop, then test everything in a sandbox before any employee sees it. Analytics close the loop after launch.

Phase 1 · First ship · ~2024

One page, three entry points

Three ways in from one screen: Quick Links for repeat tasks, direct search for anything, and a side assistant when the answer needs context. Replies cite the source document and offer the logical next step.

Phase 2 · Requester experience

Finish the task in chat

Phase 2 extended the assistant into the workflow. Users submit catalog requests, fill off-catalog forms, and receive confirmations — all without leaving the thread.

Craft

Conversation design, trust patterns, and interaction decisions

Conversational design

Three design decisions

Thumbs up / down as a tuning signal

I explored lightweight per-reply feedback—thumb up and thumb down—so we could pair qualitative signal with routing and analytics. The intent was never “vote on the model”; it was to help admins see where answers routinely missed, prioritize policy or rule updates, and later support finer-grained tuning of assistant behavior without reading every transcript.

Optional feedback
Rolls up to analytics so admins can spot weak answers without reading every thread.

How we showed sources

Trust hinged on making “where did this come from?” feel normal, not forensic. I explored how to display sources alongside the answer: readable titles, short excerpts, clear ordering, and expansion when someone needed more—enough to verify quickly, without crowding out the answer or the next step. The goal was a default people could skim, with depth on demand.

Full page versus side panel

I also explored where the assistant should live relative to the rest of the experience. A side panel next to search and Quick Links kept landing-page context in view for quick questions and shallow tasks. A dedicated full-page chat was considered for long, multi-step flows where people need uninterrupted focus and more vertical room for forms and catalog layouts. The shipped experience leaned on the hybrid shell that best matched real usage: context when you need it, room to work when a thread gets heavy.

Core design principle

Trust has to be designed in.

Buying rules are high stakes. The UI shows sources, admits uncertainty when needed, and uses safe fallbacks — not blind trust in the model.

05

Outcomes

What shipped, what we measured, and what we'd do differently

Impact

What shipped

  • Two major releases: hybrid landing page (2024), then in-chat catalog and forms (2026)
  • One consolidated entry point for answers, next steps, and checkout — policy-grounded, with explicit stop rules
  • Admin tooling (upload, routing, sandbox, analytics) shipped with v1 — not as a follow-on
  • Adopted by the Workday procurement team; extended as a reusable pattern for other assistant surfaces
Reflection

“If the rules don’t match real behavior, the ‘AI’ part doesn’t matter—the experience breaks.”

What I’d do differently: push for a live admin preview earlier. The sandbox helped, but seeing the full user-facing experience before launch would have caught more rule gaps before employees hit them.
